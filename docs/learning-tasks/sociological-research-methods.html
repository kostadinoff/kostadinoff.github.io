Practical Class Instructions:

This document contains a series of interactive scenarios designed for group work in social medicine and public health workshops. The instructor will choose a scenario for each group (or pair of groups) to solve using the principles of sociological research methods.

Reference Material

This assignment is based on the reading material: Sociological Research Methods in Medicine. All methodological justifications and critiques must reference concepts from this text.

General Instructions for Students

Each group will independently solve a distinct research problem. Your task is to:

  1. Analyze the Research Question: Determine which sociological method (survey, observation, interview or documentary analysis) best addresses it.
  2. Design the Instrument: Create a concrete data collection tool - questionnaire, interview guide, observation checklist or documentary analysis framework.
  3. The Battle (Debate): Prepare to defend your choice and critique alternative methods using evidence-based reasoning.

Scenario “The Healthcare Worker Burnout Crisis”

The Problem:: University Hospital Plovdiv has experienced a 40% increase in physician resignations. Exit interviews mention “moral injury”, “administrative burden”, and “lack of institutional support”.

Goal: Understand the drivers of burnout to plan interventions.

You choose to use:

Structured Survey Approach:

  • Your Stance: Use a standardized questionnaire (burnout assessment, stressor categories, demographics) distributed to all physicians.
  • Strengths: Systematic comparison across departments; anonymity encourages disclosure of workplace criticism; large sample size for statistical power.
  • Anticipate Critiques: How do you capture complex “moral injury” in a closed-ended scale? What about social desirability bias (fear of appearing weak)?

OR

In-Depth Interview Approach

  • Your Stance: Semi-structured interviews with a purposive sample of current and former physicians.
  • Strengths: Reveals lived experiences and causal mechanisms (the “why”); follow-up questions probe beneath surface explanations.
  • Anticipate Critiques: Is a sample of 20-30 representative of 400+ doctors? How do you handle interviewer bias and the time-intensive nature of qualitative work?

Scenario “Hospital Hand Hygiene Compliance”

The Problem: Surgical site infections have increased by 35%. Administration claims 80% hygiene compliance, but clinical evidence suggests otherwise.

Goal: Document true compliance rates and situational triggers for non-compliance.

You choose to use:

Overt Observation (Visible Observers)

  • Your Stance: Trained observers use a checklist based on WHO’s “5 Moments for Hand Hygiene” while visible to staff.
  • Strengths: High reliability; observers can clarify clinical context; easier ethical approval.
  • Anticipate Critiques: The Hawthorne Effect—staff will perform perfectly only while you are watching.

OR

Covert/Hybrid Observation

  • Your Stance: Use disguised observers or hidden cameras to capture baseline behavior, followed by overt observation for feedback.
  • Strengths: Captures “true” behavior without reactivity; reveals hidden patterns of non-compliance.
  • Anticipate Critiques: Serious ethical concerns regarding informed consent and workplace surveillance.

Scenario: Improving Maternal Health Clinic Attendance

The Problem: Roma communities in suburban Plovdiv attend prenatal care at lower rates. Administrators suspect “culture,” while community workers suspect “discrimination.”

Goal: Redesign the service based on actual barriers.

You choose to use:

Focus Group Method

  • Your Stance: Facilitate discussions with women who stopped attending to reveal collective sense-making and shared experiences.
  • Strengths: Reveals social norms and community-held beliefs; safer space for sensitive topics like discrimination.
  • Anticipate Critiques: Dominant voices may silence others; results might represent “consensus” rather than individual reality.

OR

Documentary Analysis

  • Your Stance: Audit medical records, appointment logs, and staff communications for patterns of differential treatment.
  • Strengths: Objective audit trail; immune to recall bias; identifies structural barriers in scheduling or documentation.
  • Anticipate Critiques: Records show what was documented, not the “informal” tone of voice or dismissive body language.

Scenario: Tuberculosis Treatment Adherence (Mixed Methods)

The Problem: 28% of TB patients in Plovdiv stop treatment early. Brief exit interviews lack depth.

Goal: Redesign the “Directly Observed Therapy” (DOT) program.

You choose to use:

Individual Interviews

  • Your Stance: Conduct semi-structured interviews with a purposive sample of current and former TB patients.
  • Strengths: Reveals lived experiences and causal mechanisms (the “why”); follow-up questions probe beneath surface explanations.
  • Anticipate Critiques: Is a sample of 20-30 representative of 400+ doctors? How do you handle interviewer bias and the time-intensive nature of qualitative work?

  • Strengths: Objective audit trail; immune to recall bias; identifies structural barriers in scheduling or documentation.

Evaluation Criteria:

  • Methodological Precision (30%): Instrument design follows the logic of the chosen method.
  • Conceptual Application (30%): Arguments explicitly reference the 15-minute reading material.
  • Critical Reasoning (20%): Groups identify and address valid threats (Recall Bias, Hawthorne Effect, Ecological Fallacy).
  • Communication (20%): Ability to defend and critique professionally.
